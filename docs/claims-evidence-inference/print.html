Claims, Evidence, & Inferences

1. Intro

Goals:
  • Identify claims, evidence, and inferences
  • Notice when a claim is unsupported, weakly supported, or overstated relative to the evidence
  • Make reasonable judgments about whether inferences are sound or not, relative to the evidence

2. Definitions

  • Claim: A statement that, at least in principle, can be determined to be true or false.
  • Evidence: The available body of facts or information supporting a claim. This might include data, observations, examples, citations, or documents.
  • Inference: A conclusion based on evidence and reasoning. Inferences may be reasonable, uncertain, or flat-out wrong.

3. Math Example

  • Claim: There are 4 prime numbers between 1 and 10.
  • Evidence: One can easily check that there are exactly 4 prime numbers between 1 and 10, namely, 2, 3, 5, and 7.
  • Inference: There are exactly 4 prime numbers in any 10-number interval.
  • This is not a reasonable inference and it is also flat-out wrong. In mathematics, we do not prove things by example unless we can list all possible examples. Moreover, one can readily find a counterexample - there are no prime numbers in the 10-number interval between 114 and 123.

4. Medical Example

  • Claim: Vaccinations are linked to autism.
  • Evidence: A study published by (former doctor) Andrew Wakefield in 1998.
  • Inference: We should not vaccinate our children.
  • Many believed (and some still do) that this was true. The paper, at first glance, might seem "legit".
  • The paper was later found to be fraudulent and riddled with methodological flaws. It was officially retracted by the publisher about 10 years later, several of the other authors withdrew their support, and Wakefield had his medical license revoked.

5. Physics Example

  • Claim: It is possible that regions of spacetime exist which are so densely packed with matter that the resulting gravitational force would prevent anything, even light, from ever escaping.
  • Evidence: Originally, just a mathematical theory (Einstein's Theory of General Relativity)
  • Inference: Black holes exist.
  • Einstein himself did not believe this. He thought it was a mathematical artifact that couldn't possibly reflect physical reality.
  • Since then, much indirect (gravitational effects on other visible bodies) and direct (imaging of objects such as M87* and Sagittarius A*) evidence has been accumulated.
